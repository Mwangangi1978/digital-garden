Have you ever had the urge to grab someone's head like a watermelon and peer up their nose? No? Just me? Okay, fair -- but hear me out. It's not to clear their orifices or realign their sinuses. It's to see what's going on *inside* their head.

According to Pixar's version of biology, your brain is run by a bunch of tiny people in a plush control room located somewhere behind your forehead. So essentially, Pixar is implying that the brain has outsourced its executive functions to a cheaper labor force. Ah, capitalism.

But then we're left with some pressing philosophical questions. What about the tiny people *inside* the tiny people? Is there an infinite chain of microscopic managers? Like the proverbial turtles holding up the Earth, is it little people all the way down?

This urge to figure out what's going on in other people's heads might be misplaced, though. And no, I don't mean which region of the brain controls your Spotify choices or whatever it is the prefrontal cortex gets up to once you turn 25.

I mean the *essence* of a person. Call it their soul, their beliefs, or whatever makes Sacha Baron Cohen... well, Sacha Baron Cohen.

Back in my more naive years -- which, to be honest, are still with me -- I used to think that if I just had the right facts and a well-structured argument, I could help someone see the light. But the brutal reality? You can show someone a cow, and they'll still argue it lays eggs.

Strangely, I feel this most not with people I disagree with, but with those I love. It's natural to want to peer inside the minds of those closest to us. What are they feeling? What are they going through? Do they like you back -- or just your memes?

There *are* ways to peer in. Small windows, if you will. The clearest of these is art: what they write, how they laugh, the kinds of jokes they make when no one's watching. What they say in private, in passing, in pain.

But the truth is: we can never fully know. The mind, the soul -- whatever you call it -- comes with an unbreakable privacy policy.

And maybe that's a good thing. Because when someone *does* choose to open up, it's a rare kind of magic. They didn't have to. You'd never have known otherwise. But they did.

In the end, the secrets of the soul deserve to stay secret. No amount of nose-peering will ever speed up the research. And even if we insist, we'll probably find the same thing Pixar did: It's little people all the way down. Little people shaped by friends, fears, TikTok trends -- and how often your dad does the dishes.
