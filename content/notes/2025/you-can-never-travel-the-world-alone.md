When I had finally accumulated enough consciousness to ask how a crankshaft worked, or even what one was, I watched the first episode of a car show.

Strange, because the show - starring Jeremy Clarkson, Richard Hammond, and James May (a.k.a. Captain Slow) had surprisingly little to do with automobiles. Mostly, it was three chaps racing aggressively across public roads and using obnoxious grammar like "an James" or "a egg."

This afternoon, I had the bittersweet pleasure of watching the final episode of that show, The Grand Tour - which had been preceded by Top Gear, a long-standing favorite of mine and my dad's.

At the very end, they shook hands and parted ways. Their classic cars slowly rolled off in different directions, in one of the most poetic closing scenes I've ever witnessed. As the credits rolled, hundreds of hours of their shared adventures came rushing back to me - fleeting flashes from Vietnam, Burma, the Mi Kong, and finally Zimbabwe, where they retraced their very first journey to Botswana's Kubu Island.

It reminded me of my own trek: a recently completed gap year across 10 countries and 5 continents.

It's difficult to imagine doing the same route alone, without the 96 people who made it unforgettable. Friends, strangers-turned-family, wild encounters, and quiet, shared moments. Just like with The Grand Tour, it wasn't the journey alone - it was who I shared it with.

That's why I watched Top Gear all those years. Not for the cars (though the Jaaags were a bonus), but for the chemistry. The teasing, the breakdowns, the cold beers at sunset, the reflective drives, the inside jokes over coffee.

It was never about the engines. It was about connection -- what so many of us crave deep down: to travel the world with someone.

Before I had the chance to live it, I traveled the world through them.

Today, we're encouraged to enjoy solitude. Solo dates. Journaling with a cappuccino. Embracing our own company. And yes, there's beauty in that. You should love yourself first. But let's not forget the unmatched warmth of sharing space with others. It's comforting. Joyful. Tingly. Like wearing your grandma's old wool sweater for the first time.

The best memories I have always involve people.

Running half the length of Palermo with friends for cannolis made by nuns. People-watching while eating those holy cannolis. Picking up the sense of humor of my roommate's overnight guest.

Different names, different stories; but the same core -- people.

Even the forgettable memories had people at their center. And that's the point.

> *Beautifully imperfect humanity.*

So, am I saying you shouldn't travel if you don't have someone with you?

Absolutely not.

Move. Fly. Walk. Bike. But let people be the center of it. Because the world wasn't meant to be hoarded. It was meant to be shared. That's why the Big Man Upstairs put 8 billion of us on it.

Sometimes it's a friend. Sometimes a retired vet. A busker. A child who enjoys the taste of their snot. A fellow traveler on the same long layover.

That's what keeps the world spinning:

People loving, hating, dreaming, laughing, crying, sinning and... just being.

Wherever you are in your journey:

**Safe travels**.

May you always find someone to share the view with.
