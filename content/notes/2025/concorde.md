I had to open my laptop again. She was five years old and didn't run like she used to. She was basically a desktop - I could barely use her if she wasn't plugged in. But I needed to book a flight, and I no longer had my phone; apparently, it's very easy to lose your phone when it's in your pocket while biking across a busy city.

I'd heard of this fabulous airline. P Airlines. Their livery was striking orange and black, with a tiger peekaboo-ing from the tailfin. But there was a catch. You couldn't just book a seat like with other carriers. You had to apply. And many were eager to make that flight.

I had what it took to make this flight. My suitcase was regulation perfect: black, no branding, and weighed to the decimal.

Flying with P felt essential. Not just for me; my parents were counting on it too. This wasn't an ordinary trip. Rumour had it their destination was the moon. And I *wanted* to see the moon. Sure, it had its dark side, but I'd still be there, standing on its surface. What a feeling that would be - like Neil Armstrong.

But then came the crushing news. I wasn't going.

It hurt. A clean, brutal hurt. My moon, unreachable now. Like that moment in the Jack Stauber film when you realize there's no real shelter, that sometimes the rain finds you no matter where you hide. I had done everything I was supposed to do, followed every instruction to the letter, and it still wasn't enough. But drowning in this disappointment wouldn't change anything. So I gathered up the pieces and called it education.

Then I noticed something that had always been there. I wondered why I had never thought of this before. I had waved at Concorde as it flew past my house a couple of times before. Even said "heyyy" a few times - but I don't think Concorde would have heard me from up there.

I decided to go to the airport and fly on Concorde. I had never done this before; going to the airport and saying I wanted to fly. Especially to an aircraft as beautifully engineered as Concorde. It was going to be my first time flying, so no shit I had never done this before. I had to pull the courage straight from the depths of my ass crack to do this.

I made it. I had gone all the way to the gate. For some strange reason, I had found myself at the gate. No check-in. No security either. Apparently, I was Concorde's friend already. I wasn't about to ask questions. I revelled in the good fortune.

I was nervous. I don't know why. I was at the gate. Concorde was right there in all her beauty. She wore her stripes -- blue, red, then blue again with a touch of white like a final flourish. With wings so sleek she could fly faster than the speed of sound. And onboard Concorde they always served delicious tiramisu! I couldn't look away.

When boarding came, I wanted to go last. I loved patience, and I knew there were things more important to Concorde that needed to board first. As I got closer and closer, I couldn't help but smile and make roses in the air. It was just like seeing Concorde for the first time again.

However, after the last person in front of me got in, the door started to shut. It wasn't slammed hard! It wasn't rude or inconsiderate, it was slow and gentle and kind, just like how I knew Concorde to be. The door seemed to close for an eternity; no, that's an overstatement. It was precisely 24 days. Initially, I hoped that Concorde would realize that someone was left behind, but as the days went by, my hopes dimmed more and more.

I wondered why. Why wasn't I let in? I think my ticket was only economy class, it wasn't good-looking enough. Or maybe it was because I didn't need more legroom - I was only 5'7".

I wasn't 6 foot 2. Maybe it was my misaligned teeth - I would surely bend Concorde's silver cutlery while trying to enjoy tiramisu. It may also have been my short, stubby hair whose texture was akin to steel wire - I would destroy Concorde's beautiful headrests. As the door closed, I could see my reflection, and I understood why Concorde or P Airlines didn't want me onboard.

*Of course they didn't want me.* Look at me!

I had to accept it. I would be a brute if I tried to press on. Some time back, some baboon had tried to force his way through. Concorde's door hinges were already hurt from the interaction with that goon. That was the last thing I wanted to do; hurt Concorde.

I had thought that because Concorde had seen me in person, unlike P Airlines, I would be let in. But I think being in person was much more damaging than an online application. *She could see all of me now.* Soon Concorde started to push back from the platform toward the runway. I was naive, and I felt the need to accompany Concorde on its way out instead of understanding that I was not wanted.

So I jumped off the platform, trying to get to Concorde. But as I hit the hot asphalt, both my ankles snapped. I groaned in pain. I was hurt. But this was my own doing -- not Concorde's. I crawled slowly toward the runway where Concorde was preparing to take off. I kept crawling, with my knees scraping the tarmac and the palms of my hands burning off on the 50-degree Celsius tarmac, my limp ankles being dragged along reluctantly.

But soon my double-jointed elbows buckled under the weight of my heavy heart. And I lay there burning, hurting, crying, dying. I was on the path of the next plane and I was surely going to be crushed. And surely I was. The 21-inch Airbus A380 tires began flattening my torso. The crunch was audible as my ribs shattered and pierced into my heart.

As Concorde lifted off into the fading sunlight, I lay broken on the tarmac. My chest caved inward. My lungs pancaked. Flat, airless. The roar of her engines faded into a hush.

And then:
A chime.
A cold blast from the AC.

I was back at the gate. Upright. Intact. Clutching my regulation suitcase.
Was I in a *Final Destination* movie?

"Last call for Concorde, departing Nairobi for the moon," said a voice from somewhere, from everywhere.

The door was beginning to close again. Smooth. Gentle. Inevitable.

But something felt... off.
The stripes on Concorde's fuselage were green now. There was no scent of tiramisu -- just printer toner and... *sniff sniff*... unseasoned chicken.
I was slightly taller - with silky hair. And as my tongue traced the inside of my mouth, I realized I had Invisalign.

I looked down. My suitcase felt heavier. Floating lazily.

I considered calling out. Asking something. Anything.
Maybe if they still served tiramisu?

But I just stood there, watching the door inch shut again.
Maybe my destination was never the moon.
Maybe the place I longed for was walkable.
Crawlable, even.

I might move next time.
Or not.

Maybe that's the point.
