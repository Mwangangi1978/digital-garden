> "Great art is the outward expression of an inner life in the artist, and this inner life will result in his personal vision of the world."
> -- Edward Hopper

I've been staring at this quote for some time, and it's been staring back at me like an accusation.

Edward Hopper painted seemingly lonely people. Not the romantic, aesthetic kind of lonely that looks good on Instagram with the right filter. The real kind. The kind where couples sit in diners without speaking, where people stand by windows looking at nothing in particular, where marriages exist in the same room but different universes.

Take *Nighthawks* -- his most famous painting. A couple sits at a late-night diner, not conversing, not looking at each other. The man's coffee mug is stone cold; the woman's is still steaming, suggesting he'd been waiting. And here's the kicker: there are no doors visible. No way in, and maybe most crucially, no way out. They're trapped behind that large glass window, and we're looking in at their isolation.

The models? Hopper's wife Jo and himself. The painting was a window into their life.

Windows appear in almost all of Hopper's work, and we're always looking through them at an angle, like we're peering into private moments we weren't supposed to see.

But here's the thing: Hopper often shot down attempts to project narratives about isolation onto his art. So what exactly was Hopper after? As he put it: *"I'm after me."*

He saw his art as a window into his inner life -- his moods, his memories, his perspectives. Not loneliness specifically, but himself, completely and honestly. And he understood something crucial: once you put that window out into the world, viewers bring their own inner lives to what they see. Some might see loneliness, others might see contemplation, or peace, or mystery.

Hopper had grown up extremely tall, shy, and anxious in social settings. Even his marriage to Jo had seemingly done little to alleviate his social anguish. Their relationship was volatile -- he ridiculed her artistic talents and failed to acknowledge that he couldn't have been nearly as successful without her. Jo threw her own harsh words back at him, leaving them with a marriage that was emotionally complicated.

Yet Hopper never made his art performative. He didn't turn his experiences into a show or manufacture content from his personal life. He made genuine art that left room for interpretation, that asked questions without providing easy answers. His paintings were windows -- transparent, honest, sometimes uncomfortable glimpses into a real inner life.

His art was his reality, whatever that reality was. And he trusted viewers to bring their own reality to the experience.

---

We all have yins within our yangs.

I'm not talking about the cute flaws you mention in job interviews -- *"I'm such a perfectionist!"* No, I mean the truly nasty stuff. The parts of yourself that, if I pointed them out right now, would make you react like a child:
*"Nuh uh, nuh uh."*
The stuff that would send your nervous system straight into fight-or-flight mode.

This darkness is a key part of who we are. And when channelled properly, it can become something beautiful -- perfectionism into ambition, loneliness into art, anxiety into empathy.

But sometimes the delicate balance breaks. We let it consume us, or worse, we turn it into performance.

That's when authenticity dies and pretension is born.

---

Substack and other modern platforms are excellent venues for people to share their art, their stories, their thoughts (sometimes without capital letters -- I'm still not sure why that's a thing). There are genuinely great works being published every day.

But there's a risk that Hopper never had to face:

***The temptation to perform depth instead of being deep.***

Instead of making our writing a window into ourselves -- our thoughts, values, beliefs, and yes, our demons -- we create screens. Polished surfaces that reflect what we think people want to see, maybe grounded in who we'd like to be, but ultimately opaque and vague and fake.

We write about vulnerability while being invulnerable. We preach authenticity while being authentic about nothing that matters. We become the Pharisees who drank wine but preached water.

And eventually, we lose track of what made us start writing in the first place.

---

I started this newsletter wanting to put myself out there, to use my perspectives not just to entertain but to share something meaningful. To be a window, not a screen.

I've been feeling it in myself too.

Writing advice I sometimes haven't used. Speaking with clarity I don't always live by. Building paragraphs that feel like windows, but are really projection screens for the person I'm trying to be, not the person I actually am.

And if I kept going like that, it wouldn't just be hypocritical.
It'd be pointless.

Because what's the point of writing if I'm not using it to actually learn?
What's the point of sharing if I'm not still asking?

So I'm making a change.
Less of the performative depth.
More of the weird questions. The simple fascinations. The little stories that teach us how strange and wonderful the world really is.

---

Small things. Big ideas. Silly packaging. Real substance.

That's the direction I'm headed -- a new theme, maybe a new name for this stack. Still me, just... more me.

I won't over-explain it here. You'll see it.
And hopefully, you'll feel it.

---

So here's my question for you:
**In what ways are you showing a screen instead of a window?**
**Where in your life are you performing rather than being?**

And more importantly:
**What can be done about it?**

I'll leave that open-ended, like Hopper would have. The best art doesn't give you answers -- it gives you better questions.
