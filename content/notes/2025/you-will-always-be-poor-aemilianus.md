> "For everyone who has, more will be given, and they shall have enough and to spare. But from those who have not, even what they have will be taken away."

Take a second. Who said this?

F. Scott Fitzgerald? J.F. Kennedy? Martial, some Roman poet with a grudge against the working class? Jesus Christ?

Believe it or not, the answer is: all of the above, or at least, something hauntingly close.

I'll admit, I was confused too. Someone brought this up in a conversation about how modern society seems rigged, and I couldn't stop thinking about it. Were they all just casually advocating for the poor to get poorer while the rich hoard more riches? Is this divine endorsement of late-stage capitalism with a literary twist?

Let's rewind and see how deep this rabbit hole goes.

F. Scott Fitzgerald, in *The Great Gatsby*, tells us:

> "The rich get richer and the poor get - children."

Then there's Martial - the Roman poet, some 2000 years earlier, who wrote in one of his epigrams:

> "You will always be poor if you are poor, Aemilianus. Nowadays wealth is given only to the rich."

Both point to the same uncomfortable truth: those with more money, looks, status, good WiFi often attract even more. And not just in financial matters. Ever notice how the tallest trees get more sunlight and grow even stronger? Or how people with one viral tweet suddenly become Twitter philosophers overnight?

Is it fair? Absolutely not. Does it happen everywhere? Absolutely yes.

Now here's where it gets really wild.

Jesus Christ -- yes, that Jesus -- says in Matthew 13:12:

> "Whoever has will be given more, and they will have an abundance. Whoever does not have, even what they have will be taken away."

Then JFK enters the chat with:

> "To those whom much is given, much is required."

Okay, different vibe. More "with great power comes great responsibility" than "let them eat cake." But still playing in the same arena, the idea that having leads to more having.

So what's going on here? Was Jesus quietly endorsing trickle-up economics? Should we expect the next parable to involve hedge funds and invisible hands?

Not quite.

In biblical context, Jesus wasn't talking about wealth or privilege. He was referring to understanding, growth, and spiritual insight. It's about receptiveness. Basically, if you're open and curious, you'll keep learning and growing. But if you close yourself off, if you think you already know everything, even what you think you understand might slip away.

It's less "the rich get richer" and more "the open get opener."

But here's what's fascinating: in the hands of poets and politicians, the line mutates. It becomes a mirror, reflecting whatever system you're looking at - economic, social, spiritual, romantic. The same pattern appears everywhere because maybe that's just how momentum works in the universe.

People in motion stay in motion. Trees in the sun grow faster.
And yes, sometimes people in love seem to attract even more love.

But maybe the real secret, the one both Jesus and Martial were circling in their own way, is this:

**The true poverty isn't just a lack of money or status. It's closing yourself off.**
It's deciding you've seen enough, learned enough, felt enough.

The people who keep growing in wisdom, love, understanding - are the ones who stay curious. Those who keep their hands open. Who remain willing to be surprised by what they don't know. Who ask for help. Who say "I don't know" without shame.

That's where abundance begins.
Not with what you have, but with how ready you are to receive what you don't have yet.

As for Aemilianus? He probably stayed poor because the system was rigged.
But here's the twist: there was never an Aemilianus at all. He was Martial's fictional stand-in, a poetic punching bag for his frustration with Roman society.

Still, Martial tried. He turned his bitterness into literature instead of silence. And now, thousands of years later, here we are talking about him.

That is abundance.
