I've been lying here for what seems like weeks. It could be longer. I don't really keep track.

Initially, I thought I was in a mattress store because the decor was very mid, there was no customer service and it was oddly comfortable. No alarms. No need to go to the toilet. No need for new laundry, although something was beginning to stink real bad.

No more *how are you doing* texts. Just being still.

I sometimes feel like I am just in a dream. Or in a very expensive flat that I cannot afford but was somehow allowed to rent once and never got evicted from.

No rent.
No feelings or emotions.
And absolutely no neighbors.

Except for him.
The ant.

He's got 8 legs, 2 heads, a singular antennae and a voice like a head teacher. Not annoying, just set on it.

Which was weird 'cause I don't remember ants had features like that.

He walks up my arm every morning. Like clockwork. I call him Mr. A.
He calls me "Boss." Which feels wrong.
I haven't bossed anything in months.

He is the messenger of the world I left behind. My antennae to the sky. He tells me about elections, breakups and album drops.

At first it was fun. I would ask Mr. A for gossip, like:

> "Did Lucy finally leave her man?"
>
> "She didn't. At last, though, her man broke down in tears. In the open."
>
> "Well done."

His updates, however, have been a little less newsworthy. Quieter. Softer.

This morning Mr. A arrived with something heavy wedged between his mandibles.

> "Boss," he began, sitting on my collarbone. "There's a boy."
>
> "Aren't there always?", I mumbled.
>
> "He leaped from GreyStone Towers' 12th floor. Just like that."
>
> "Oh."
>
> "They say he was always smiling. Joked a lot. Took the most group photos."
>
> "Classic."
>
> "Then one day, poof! He stopped replying. No a word, not a note or a goodbye. Only a gust of wind."

I felt something shift. Not guilt. But something thinner. Like a shoelace loosening.

> "According to his best friend, they had a conversation the previous evening. Claimed he sounded fine. Jollier than usual even. Like he was about to receive his paycheck the following day."

That word bothers me. *"Fine."*
It's what people say when they're on fire.

> "Anyway," Mr. A continued,
>
> "His name was the same as yours. Strange, huh?"

I didn't answer.

He gave me a massive side-eye. His pupils dilated -- well metaphorically.

> "Boss... he was *you*!"

And in that moment, it clicked.

It was never a mattress.
The cozy silence wasn't cozy.
The darkness wasn't a vibe.

And it explained why the apartment had no windows.

I wasn't on a break.
I was six feet under.

And Mr. A?
He wasn't a messenger.

He was a mourner.
A feeder.
A daily visitor.

> "Why do you keep coming?" I asked in a thin voice.
>
> "Because you taste like someone no one checked on," he mumbled as he chewed on my decomposing neck.

---

This short story is dedicated to the close friend I lost along the way.

Sometimes there are no signs. No countdown. No letter.
Sometimes the silence *is* the signal.
And sometimes, the people who made us laugh the hardest were hurting the most.

**Check in.**
Even if they say they're *"fine."*
